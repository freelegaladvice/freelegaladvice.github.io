Grace, a 34-year-old mother of three from Lusaka, had always dreamed of expanding her small tailoring business. After hearing about Jumo Loans from a friend, she applied for a small loan through her mobile phone. Within hours, the funds were disbursed to her account. Her story exemplifies how micro-finance can turn a side hustle into a sustainable source of income, lifting entire households out of poverty.

David's Agri-Venture: Boosting Food Security Through Innovation

Agriculture is a critical sector in Zambia, but smallholder farmers often face challenges like climate change, poor infrastructure, and limited access to finance. David, a 42-year-old farmer from the Copperbelt Province, wanted to invest in drip irrigation technology to conserve water and increase crop yields. After being rejected by a local bank, he turned to Jumo Loans. The process was straightforward: he applied via USSD on his basic mobile phone, and based on his transaction history, he was approved for a loan. With the funds, David implemented the irrigation system, which helped him grow vegetables year-round, even during dry seasons. His productivity soared, and he now supplies fresh produce to local schools and supermarkets. David's success not only improved his family's livelihood but also contributed to regional food security, showcasing the ripple effect of strategic investment in agriculture.

She applied for a loan to cover her first year's tuition, using her smartphone to complete the application. "This loan was more than money; it was an investment in my dreams," she reflects. Her story highlights how accessible credit can empower youth to pursue education and break intergenerational cycles of poverty.

Leo's journey demonstrates how fintech can fuel entrepreneurship in the digital age, enabling youth to participate in the global economy.

Gender inequality remains a barrier to progress in many parts of the world, including Zambia. Women often have less access to financial resources, education, and entrepreneurial opportunities. Maria's Market Stall: Building a Legacy of Independence

Maria, a 50-year-old widow from Livingstone, used to sell vegetables at a local market but struggled to make ends meet. She dreamed of expanding her stall to include dried fish and household goods, which had higher profit margins. After attending a financial literacy workshop that partnered with Jumo, Maria learned how to apply for a loan through her phone. She received a loan that allowed her to diversify her products and increase her inventory. Within six months, her income had doubled, and she was able to send her grandchildren to school. Maria's success has inspired other women in her community to explore entrepreneurship. "I am now the boss of my life," she says proudly. Her story underscores the transformative power of financial inclusion in advancing gender equality and fostering economic resilience among women.

Supporting Female Farmers: The Cooperative That Changed a Community

In rural Zambia, women are often the backbone of agricultural production but face significant barriers to scaling their operations. A group of women in the Eastern Province formed a cooperative to pool resources and sell crops collectively. However, they needed funding to purchase better storage facilities and transportation. Through Jumo's group lending option, the cooperative secured a loan that enabled them to buy a small truck and build a storage shed. This investment reduced post-harvest losses and allowed them to access larger markets in urban areas. The cooperative's members have seen their incomes rise, and they now advocate for other women to join them. This example shows how technology-driven lending can strengthen community-based initiatives and promote sustainable development.
